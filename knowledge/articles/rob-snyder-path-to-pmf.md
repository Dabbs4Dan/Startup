# The Path to Product-Market Fit — Rob Snyder (Harvard iLabs)

> Open-sourced 91-slide workshop deck. Rob spent 2 years in "the pain cave" pre-PMF despite McKinsey + HBS + reading every book. After going $0–$4M ARR in 2 years and taking 10+ companies $0–$1M ARR, he claims most pre-PMF suffering is *unnecessary* — caused by founders using the wrong approach. The deck is his alternative.

---

## Core thesis

**Demand rules everything; you don't create it, you find it. Business is just a system to replicate a single case study with intense demand. The only way to find that case study is to *sell* it (theoretically at first), debug what makes prospects say "hell yes," and unfold from there — not pivot, not interview, not theorize.** Selling is the *only* mechanism that exposes real demand because economic transactions activate a different part of the brain than social ones.

---

## Frameworks

### The Pain Cave (and the path out)

- **What:** The 12–24+ month pre-PMF suffering most founders endure.
- **Why it happens:** (1) building things people don't want despite "discovery," (2) trying to solve a million startup variables at once (beachhead, persona, value prop, MVP, vision…), (3) working hard on things that don't matter while feeling like you're doing all the right things.
- **Path out:** stop trying to do things "the right way" / "validate hypotheses" / etc. Just sell whatever someone will buy. Figure out the business by trying to sell and deliver.
- **Rob's own path:** *I was the product (in Gsheets) until $100k ARR. No website or customer login until $1M ARR. Just a 4-slide sales pitch until ~$3M ARR. I onboarded the first 300 customers 1:1.*

### Demand vs Supply (the master distinction)

The single load-bearing distinction in the deck. Most founders live entirely on the supply side and wonder why nothing works.

| | **Demand** | **Supply** |
|---|---|---|
| Where most founders spend time | Nobody focuses on it | Where everyone spends their time |
| Control | You have **zero** control | Everything you control |
| Existence | Exists whether or not you exist | Only exists because of you |
| What it is | What a person is trying to achieve | How you help them achieve it |
| Your role | Find it & harness it | Respond to it |
| Causal direction | **Causes** supply to be relevant | Only relevant if it responds to demand |
| Founder experience | Makes business simple & intuitive | Makes business complex & impossible |
| Outcome | Where you find PMF | Where you find pain & ambiguity |
| Force direction | **PULLS** your business into existence | PUSH (uphill if no demand, downhill if demand) |

**Hard truths:**
- Nobody wants your product.
- Found a 10/10 pain point? Still doesn't mean there's demand.
- Found a big problem? Still doesn't mean there's demand.
- People excited about your product? Still doesn't mean there's demand.

**At the atomic level**, demand is:
- One person who needs to accomplish something
- Why she needs to accomplish *this*, vs everything else she could do
- What options she considers for accomplishing this thing

**The Asana model.** Imagine her project management board is perfectly accurate. A million projects in backlog; only 1–2 prioritized at any time. When prioritized, she "gets real," adds detail, considers approaches. Your job:
1. Which project does my product fit into?
2. **WHEN** does she prioritize this project vs others? What **causes** that?
3. When prioritized, what options does she consider? What's the *shape* of the project, her goals & anti-goals?

### The Bottoms-Up PMF Model (one real customer, not abstractions)

> *"Let's use the fewest fancy MBA abstract concepts possible — and start from ONE REAL CUSTOMER, & build a model from there."*

Top-down (niches, personas, market segments, hypotheses, slogans, trends) **sounds right** but is the pain cave in practice. Bottoms-up actually works.

**The model:**
- **One Case Study** = one real person's journey: prospective customer → actual customer → successful customer (renew/repeat)
- Demand = what Melissa was trying to accomplish
- Supply = how we helped Melissa accomplish it
- Your business = a system to replicate that one case study (clone, copy + paste)
- Why ONE case study? Because N case studies is N² as hard. And one is hard enough.
- Why one real person? Because you never sell to 1,000 at once. Always 1×1. There is no "scale transformation."

**PMF is measured by RETENTION, not (just) GROWTH.**

### The 6-Part Case Study (the unit of business)

A case study is a story in 6 parts. The first 4 parts are demand (stuff you don't control); the last 2 are supply (what you control).

| # | Part | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Project** | What project was this person trying to accomplish? (Think their Asana board.) |
| 2 | **Context** | Why was this project on their critical path, vs every other project they could have prioritized? |
| 3 | **Options** | What options did they consider to accomplish this project? |
| 4 | **Results** | What did success look like to them? How did they know they'd accomplished it, no going back? |
| 5 | **How** | How exactly did they complete the project — with help from your product? |
| 6 | **What** | What did they buy? (Packaging, positioning, pricing, offer.) |

**Worked example (Waffle / SOC 2):**
- Project: CTO wanted to get QA off their plate so they could ship faster with confidence
- Context: struggling with quality, tried simple tools, realized scope of work
- Options: dedicated QA team, outsourcing, leveraging AI
- Results: QA under control in a few weeks, ½ the cost of an in-house hire
- How: AI agents managed by a human QA architect (us)
- What: $X/month with a 1-month money-back guarantee

### The 5 Levels of PMF

| Level | What you have | Founder signal |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No customer case study worth replicating | Pre-revenue or no usable wins |
| 2 | Have one, but can't consistently replicate | A few happy customers, no system |
| 3 | Can replicate, but not "hell yes" every time | Consistent customers same way + same pitch + high conversion |
| 4 | Can replicate "hell yes," need a growth lever | Customers consistently renew / upgrade / refer |
| 5 | "Hell yes" + growth lever, holding on for dear life | "You'll know when you're at 5" |

### Path to PMF — what matters by stage

| Stage | Pipeline | Sales Process | Delivery / CS |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Get Real (0→~10)** | Founder magic | Theoretical case study or beg | "Hell yes" by any means necessary. Don't do things that scale. |
| **Case Study Sales (10→~50)** | Founder magic | **Systematize.** Replicate the case study. Target 60%+ "hell yes," close on good-fit buyers. | **Systematize.** Bullet train to "hell yes." Retention drives product development. |
| **Find GTM Fit (50+)** | **Find ONE primary scalable, cost-efficient channel.** This is the hard part now. | Debug / enhance when scale breaks it | Debug / enhance when scale breaks it |
| **Don't Get Distracted (100+)** | Do more of what works. **Defend the case study.** | Do more of what works | Do more of what works |

### The Case Study Sales Pitch

> *"The demo isn't you showing your product to a prospect & trying to persuade them to buy it. The demo is you sitting on the same side of the table as your prospect, exploring* a similar company's case study *and letting that story persuade your prospect to buy."*

**The 3-meeting B2B process** (often collapsed for B2C):
1. **Meeting 1: Case Study Pitch** — identify what they need to accomplish + how they think about options & timeline. Use the case study even if you don't have one yet.
2. **Meeting 2: Deep Dive** — implementation walkthrough; recap, why-now, key workflows, implementation plan, internal process to get it done.
3. **Meeting 3: Decision** — recap, remaining objections, contract review, schedule onboarding (use as forcing function).

**The actual call flow** (demand-first, supply-last):
- "What caused you to take the meeting? What's going on in your world?" → DEMAND
- "Usually I hear from people in your situation they have this one massive challenge… sound familiar?" → DEMAND
- "This is how John (in your situation) thought about his options… how are you thinking about yours?" → DEMAND
- "John chose X option, here's what he accomplished… sound valuable?" → DEMAND
- "Here's how we helped John… worth seeing if we can help you too?" → SUPPLY

### Unfolding vs Pivoting

**Pivoting** = mechanical direction-change. Implication: go back to zero, first principles. Bad idea. Leads to "pivot hell" + extra time in the pain cave.

**Unfolding** = evolution that *compounds* knowledge. Given current knowledge & product, what is the evolution that builds on **all our advantages** + **everything we've learned** and makes us stronger, simpler, more coherent, more in-line with demand?

**The 5 Case Study Unfolding Patterns** (ordered by business impact, not by what founders tend to lean toward):

| # | Pattern | Example | Do this when |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Reframe to true demand** | "Simplify AWS" → "Build SOC 2 Compliant Products" | You find out why people *really* buy, or *who* has intense demand |
| 2 | **Intensify demand** | "Build SOC 2 Compliant Products" → "SOC 2 in 1 Weekend" | You feel slight pull, know there's something deeper |
| 3 | **Simplify / clarify the story** | "Alternative to Vanta" → "Partnering with Vanta" | Pull early in case study, but they ask questions later showing they don't get it |
| 4 | **Remove friction from supply** | "Replatform your stack" → "Wrap your stack (open-source, AWS-native)" | Objections around "how it works" or no action despite clear demand |
| 5 | **Remove friction for next step** | "Catch up next week" → "Walk you through the open-source version" | Good meetings + clear demand, but drop-off at next step |

> Founders **lean toward** the right side of this list (supply-side fixes). Higher business impact lives on the **left** (demand-side).

### Outreach (5–10 calls/week)

**Two cheat codes:**
1. What's the message **only you** can send to **only them**?
2. What's in it for **them**?

**Example:** *"I'm just getting back from filming Sesame Street, and would love to learn more about your company's marketing plan, plus share what I'm hearing from other brands… Here's my calendar link."*

**Practical:**
- LinkedIn + email is usually enough early
- Can use automation (Sales Nav + Dripify, Apollo) but don't *sound* like automation
- Goal: 5–10 sales calls per week
- Then debug the case study + sales + delivery from those calls
- Eventually a *scalable* pipeline source emerges based on the case study

### Delivery: founder-magic until V2

Sales gives you the chance to figure out delivery & PMF. **Retention is the marker of PMF.** People at "equilibrium 2" don't want to / can't go back.

**Rob's V1 delivery (his last company, $0–$125k):**
- Logged into customers' applicant software, downloaded past applicants to CSV
- Copied a Gsheets template, added applicants
- Uploaded 50 contacts/store/day, sent mass recruiting messages, chatted w/ candidates
- Manual button click to email recruiter when interested
- Manually emailed weekly customer reports
- Bi-weekly Zoom check-ins
- 8am–7pm for 3 months, eng watching on Zoom
- ❌ No customer login. ❌ No website. 💰 3-slide sales deck. ⚙ Eng shipped just enough to keep him alive.

**What the customer experienced:** 1:1 personalized onboarding with a founder; simple email reports ("you don't have to learn some new system!"); Rob's direct phone number to yell at him when things broke.

**Principles:**
- Customers buy a project; your product is *nested* within it
- They don't care what % is product vs elbow-grease
- **FORCE customers to project success by ANY MEANS NECESSARY.** They don't have an option — they're going to get the result.
- Eventually nail self-serve. **But only after you've figured out what "hell yes" post-implementation looks like** — that's the leading indicator of retention/upsell.
- **You need far less product than you think.**

## Mental models / principles

- **Demand is invisible.** You can't tell from looking at a product what demand is. — *Rob Snyder*
- **Demand is action, not pain.** Life is a series of pain points you do nothing about. Demand is about ACTION, sometimes caused by pain. — *Rob Snyder*
- **The customer's perception of "demand → supply" is the entire game.** Demand makes value props relevant or not, not the other way around. — *Rob Snyder*
- **One case study can take you to $1M, $10M, even $100M+.** — *Rob Snyder*
- **Pivoting throws away everything you've learned. Unfolding compounds it.** — *Rob Snyder*
- **Retention is the marker of PMF, not (just) growth.** — *Rob Snyder*
- **Sell to learn, deliver manually, debug toward "hell yes."** — *Rob Snyder*
- **Asking for real money is an "economic transaction"; everything short of that is "social."** Social transactions are forgiving, generous, encouraging — and lying to you. — *Parker Ence (cofounder, Jump)*
- **You'll know when you're at PMF Level 5.** Don't second-guess prior levels — they have clear markers. — *Rob Snyder*
- **The whole point is to serve customers to achieve what matters to them. It's not about you or how you think the world should work.** — *Rob Snyder*

## Tactics & playbooks

- **Draft a *theoretical* case study before you have a real one.** Use it to sell. Debug it from real call feedback. Don't wait for "validated" inputs.
- **Schedule 5–10 sales calls per week.** This is the volume needed to learn anything.
- **Review every call for 15–30+ minutes.** Hold a weekly reflection to debug toward "hell yes" — from specific customer examples, never from theory.
- **Make the call a conversation, not a presentation.** Thank them for objections. Ask "help me understand…"
- **Use the Asana question:** "When you buy, what's your Asana project?" If you can't answer this, you don't understand demand yet.
- **Write outreach using two cheat codes:** what only-you-can-send-to-only-them + what's-in-it-for-them.
- **Onboard the first ~300 customers yourself, 1:1.** Then systematize.
- **Force customers to success by any means necessary.** Manual delivery in V1. Don't automate until you know what "hell yes" looks like post-implementation.
- **Order the unfolding patterns by demand-side impact first** (Reframe → Intensify → Simplify → Supply friction → Next-step friction). Most founders default to the right side; left side is higher leverage.
- **Don't pivot unless absolutely necessary.** Try to unfold first.
- **Defend the case study at scale.** Once you have it, the post-PMF risk is distraction.

## Counterintuitive claims

- **"Nobody wants your product."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: if I find a real pain point and a real problem, demand exists. Their argument: even a 10/10 pain point doesn't equal demand. Demand is action, not pain — "life is a series of pain points you do nothing about."
- **"You don't create demand."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: founders create new categories, evangelize, generate demand. Their argument: demand exists whether or not you exist. You find it and harness it. The entire startup industry teaches the opposite.
- **"Customer interviews are insufficient."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view (and most startup curricula, including Techstars): empathy interviews, JTBD analysis, "get out of the building" → you'll find what people want. Their argument: you'll still build things people don't want, because **only money-on-the-line activates the economic-transaction part of the brain** (per Parker Ence). Interviews are social transactions; they lie. You must *sell* to truly find demand.
- **"Pivoting is bad. Unfold instead."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: pivot when something isn't working — Lean Startup canon. Their argument: pivoting throws away compounding knowledge and sends you to "pivot hell." Unfolding evolves the case study while preserving everything you've learned.
- **"You need far less product than you think."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: build the MVP, then v2, then platform, then scale. Their argument: he ran the V1 of his company manually for 3 months in Google Sheets, no customer login until $1M ARR, just a 3-slide deck.
- **"Retention is the marker of PMF, not growth."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: PMF = "hockey stick growth." Their argument: growth without retention is leakage. The true marker is "they're at equilibrium 2 and can't / don't want to go back."
- **"ONE case study can take you to $100M+."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: scale = serving more personas, more verticals, more use cases. Their argument: variance kills businesses. N case studies is N² as hard. Unfold the one you have.
- **"Founders lean toward supply-side debugging; demand-side is higher impact."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: tighten the pitch, polish the deck, lower friction. Their argument: those are the *right side* of the unfolding patterns. The left side (reframe demand, intensify it) is where the real moves live, but founders avoid it because it feels like throwing away work.

## Open questions

- **B2C applicability.** Rob says the case study + 3-meeting model is B2B-tuned and B2C usually closes in convo 1, but doesn't go deep on B2C-specific adaptations.
- **When does "manual delivery" stop working?** Rob personally onboarded 300 customers. Is that the threshold? Earlier? Later? Industry-dependent?
- **Does this scale beyond founder-led?** "Founder magic" carries pipeline + delivery in stages 1–2. He acknowledges it must be systematized at 50+ customers, but the deck doesn't fully prescribe the handoff.
- **Where does the case study come from initially?** "Theoretical" or "beg" — but how do you *generate* the first theoretical case study? Implicit answer: pick someone you know is the buyer profile and write what their journey *would* look like. Light on specifics.
- **What is a "growth lever"?** PMF Level 4 → 5 hinges on this; the deck doesn't define what counts.
