# Understand Your Customers (Empathy Interviews + Jobs to Be Done)

## Core thesis
Early-stage startups must understand customer problems from the *market's* perspective — not the founder's assumption — before building solutions. Empathy interviews discover what customers actually need; the Jobs to Be Done lens turns those discoveries into actionable design inputs.

## Frameworks

### Empathy Interview Framework
- **What:** Open-ended customer conversation focused on *their* experiences and problems, not your pitch.
- **Components:** Preparation · rapport-building · open-ended questioning · active listening.
- **Goal:** Capture three dimensions of customer needs — **functional** (what they're trying to do), **social** (how they want to be perceived doing it), **emotional** (how they want to feel).

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
- **What:** Synthesis lens — translate empathy interview output into "the job the customer hires the product to do."
- **Process:** Extract patterns from interviews; identify functional, social, and emotional progress dimensions from the customer's perspective.

### Customer Demand Strategy ("pull" messaging)
- **Approach:** Use customer language and imagery — let messaging *pull* customers rather than push the product.
- **Elements:** Customer/problem understanding · their language · their imagery · product-message resonance.

## Tactics
- Conduct empathy interviews **before** product development — not as a validation step after building.
- Ask open-ended questions that invite **storytelling** about specific past experiences ("walk me through the last time you…").
- Use the Empathy Interview Planner to identify what you don't yet know before going in.
- Synthesize interview transcripts to find patterns — don't analyze in isolation.
- "Get out of the building" — internal theorizing is the failure mode.

## Counterintuitive claims
- **"Your solution may work perfectly and still fail"** if the social context is overlooked — functional fit isn't enough.
- **The problem definition matters more than the initial solution approach** — most early-stage failures are problem misframing, not solution execution.
