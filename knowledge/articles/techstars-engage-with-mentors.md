# Engage With Mentors

## Core thesis
Founders waste mentor meetings by **describing what they do** instead of articulating their *problems*. Productive mentorship comes from problem-specific questions, reading nonverbal cues for engagement, and choosing mentors who challenge rather than affirm.

## Frameworks

### Mentor Meeting Optimization (6 components)
1. **Read mentors** — recognize nonverbal disengagement signals.
2. **Redirect conversations** — pull tangents back to the core business problem.
3. **Ask better questions** — problem-focused inquiry, not pitch recital.
4. **Build relationships** — leverage broader knowledge, not just immediate business questions.
5. **Choose challenging mentors** — authentic perspective beats affirming validation.
6. **Apply insights** — translate the meeting into operational changes, not just notes.

## Tactics
- Watch for nonverbal cues — **watch-checking, fidgeting, body language** — to detect mentor disengagement and adjust in real time.
- Redirect off-topic conversation back to your core business problem.
- Replace pitch-style intros with **specific, problem-focused questions**.
- Build relationships *beyond* the immediate ask — gives access to mentor's broader leadership insight over time.
- Seek mentors who **challenge** you rather than ones who only validate. Affirmation feels good and teaches little.

## Counterintuitive claims
- **"Entrepreneurs tend to waste time during mentor meetings describing what they do and failing to articulate their problems."** Pitch-focused preparation is *less* valuable than problem clarity in this context. The mentor isn't an investor; treat the meeting as a working session.
- **Choose mentors who'll disagree with you.** The instinct is to find people who believe in the vision. The leverage is in finding people who'll challenge it.
