# EQ for Entrepreneurs

## Core thesis
**Emotional intelligence is 2–4x more important than IQ for entrepreneurial success** — and it's a learnable skill, not a fixed trait. Investors evaluate EQ before product or pitch. Self-awareness is the rate-limiter most founders haven't measured.

## Frameworks

### Goleman's 4-Quadrant EQ Model
1. **Self-Awareness** — your strengths, weaknesses, core values, emotional triggers
2. **Self-Management** — staying calm under pressure; managing emotions vs. being driven by them
3. **Social Awareness** — empathy, reading room dynamics
4. **Social Skills** — feedback, trust, leadership, perspective-taking

### Johari Window (1955)
Four quadrants of self-knowledge:
- **Open Self** — known to you and others
- **Hidden Self** — known to you, hidden from others
- **Unknown** — neither you nor others see it
- **Blind Spots** — others see, you don't

**Goal:** Reduce hidden self via vulnerability; move blind spots into the open box via feedback.

### Feet on the Floor (30-second grounding)
Pre-meeting reset: close eyes, ease mind, prepare. Use before stressful encounters.

### Red / Yellow / Green Check-in (Jerry Colonna, Reboot.io)
Pre-meeting team presence exercise:
- 🟢 **Green** — in flow, calm, ready
- 🟡 **Yellow** — some anxiety/distraction
- 🔴 **Red** — significant emotional upset

**Action:** Address red-state team members first before proceeding with the agenda.

### The Three Kinds of Feedback
1. **Appreciation** — recognition for helpful behavior
2. **Evaluation** — non-judgmental coaching feedback
3. **Coaching** — future-looking, requested, designed for improvement

### Spot Coaching Question Progression (from "The Coaching Mindset")
Sequential questions for growth conversations:
1. What's on your mind?
2. And what else?
3. What's the real challenge for you?
4. What do you want?
5. If you say yes to this, what are you saying no to?
6. What was most useful to you?

### Cognitive Triangle
**Thoughts ↔ Feelings ↔ Actions** all interconnect. Emotion can't be removed from leadership decisions — only understood.

### Growth Mindset vs. Fixed Mindset
- **Fixed:** unwilling to examine other perspectives, avoids feedback, reactive, judgmental, inflexible.
- **Growth:** open to feedback, hypothesis-driven, willing to be wrong, asks powerful questions.

## Tactics
- **Build self-awareness baseline:** list everything you know about yourself, then share with someone who knows you well to identify blind spots.
- Take **personality assessments** (some carry costs, but signal value).
- Practice **Feet on the Floor** before high-stakes meetings.
- Implement **Red/Yellow/Green** check-ins at the start of meetings.
- Frame questions positively: *"what's working and how do we do more?"* beats *"what's not working?"*
- **Practice saying "I was wrong" repeatedly** until it's as comfortable as saying hello.
- Ask for **lightweight feedback** first — pick non-triggering topics from trusted sources to build the feedback habit.
- Resist giving unsolicited advice when coaching — **lean back, listen**.
- Ask "**what else?**" — first answers rarely reveal the actual issue.
- Hold **unconditional positive regard** — coach with empathy and emotional boundaries.

## Counterintuitive claims
- **Only 10–15% of people actually have high self-awareness, despite 95% believing they do.** The gap is the leverage point.
- **Fear is not a barrier — it's the default.** "A great company is built by people that felt fear and worked through it." Founders trying to eliminate fear are solving the wrong problem.
- **"The number one thing that highly intelligent people have in common is that they're wrong, a lot."** Intelligence and rightness are separate.
- **Your business plan is necessarily wrong** because it's made with ~5% of available information; the other 95% is unknown. Plan accordingly.
- **Investors evaluate EQ *before* product/pitch quality.** "Is this someone I would work for?" is the first question.
- **Asking "what do you want?" can help people discover desires they weren't consciously aware of.** Don't assume they already know.
- **Your job as a leader is not to have all the answers — it's to have powerful questions.**

## Examples
- **Mike Tyson:** *"Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the mouth."*
- **Harvard Business Review:** 95% of people report high self-awareness; only 10–15% actually possess it.
- **Seven fear buckets entrepreneurs face (HBR):** financial security · funding ability · personal ability/self-esteem · idea potential · social esteem threats · venture execution · opportunity costs.
- **Weak vs. powerful question:** *"Who thinks we should start charging beta customers?"* (yes/no, closes thinking) vs. *"What might happen if we start charging our customers?"* (opens thinking).
