# Rob Snyder — Where to Find Startup Ideas (Physics of Startups #5)

> 54-minute interview building on the Path-to-PMF deck. Where the deck answered *"how do you reach PMF once you have a candidate?"*, this answers the upstream question: ***"where do startup ideas come from in the first place, and how do you know when you've found one?"*** Rob introduces the **Pull Framework** — his operational definition of demand — and the **Wave / Dam / River** taxonomy of where to look. Brutal critique of discovery interviews and design partners. **This is corpus-critical for the founder right now** — directly addresses his "I'm struggling to find a worthy Melissa" bottleneck.

---

## Core thesis

**Most startup advice tells you to look for the wrong things in the wrong places.** Pain points, "people who would benefit," "people who want a product," "underserved market niches" — all directionally right but actually wrong, and they lead to the pain cave. **What you're actually looking for is *pull*** — a person with an unavoidable project, blocked by insufficient options, who would rip a janky version of your product out of your hands. **Where you find pull depends on whether you're surfing a wave, breaking a dam, or working a river.** And almost everything labeled "discovery interviews" or "design partners" is between LARPing-as-founder and being productive — closer to the LARP side.

---

## Frameworks

### The Pull Framework — Rob's operational definition of demand

> *"Instead of looking for kind of static problems and pain points, we are looking for the actions people are actually trying to take, their priorities — and specifically a priority that they are trying to act on but are blocked, and their existing options for acting on that priority are not good enough such that when we offer our option, they pull the product out of our hands."*

The 4 criteria:

| # | Criterion | What you're looking for |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **A project** | A specific thing the person is actively trying to accomplish (their Asana, their priority list) |
| 2 | **Why it's unavoidable** | They cannot *not* do this project right now — there's a forcing function |
| 3 | **Options considered** | What other paths they're looking at to accomplish this |
| 4 | **Why those options have serious limitations** | The existing options don't get them there |

When all four are present, your offer (whatever its current state) gets *pulled* out of your hands. **This is product-agnostic** — it's about a person trying to accomplish something, not about your product. *Their project is upstream of your product.*

### The "It would be weird if..." test

> *"Given what's on most people's to-do list, it would be weird if they bought your product. But some people — given what's on their to-do list — it would be weird if they DIDN'T buy your product."*

The framing flip-test for unavoidability. If you can't say "it would be weird if this person didn't buy" → you don't have the right Melissa yet.

### The Janky Product Test (the white-hot center of demand)

> *"Our ideal customer is the one who has such intense pull that they will rip the janky version of the product out of our hands."*

The signal of true early demand isn't "would they pay" or "do they like the demo." It's: **would they take the broken, half-built, embarrassing version of this *right now* because their pain is that intense?**
- This won't scale to $10M ARR — that's the point. You're hunting the *white-hot center* of demand.
- *"What most people think it's about: convincing a bunch of people who have lukewarm or no demand that they should try our product. That leads to all the downstream — oh, we need to hire 40 developers, oh, we need a perfect product. No. We have to find the person who desperately needs help."*

### The Wave / Dam / River taxonomy of *where to look*

The principles (look for pull) hold in all three; the *sourcing tactics* differ.

**🌊 Wave** — an emerging set of new projects appearing on people's to-do lists, usually triggered by new technology (e.g., AI agents).
- **Where to go:** the frontier. *"You've got to be in SF tinkering with AI agents alongside other people tinkering with AI agents."*
- **What to do:** build something *for yourself* at the frontier, show it to others at the frontier. They go *"hell yes I need that."* Then in 6–12 months, every software company is implementing V1 of this and they need it too.
- **Risk:** uncertain whether the wave will form, hit, or pass you. Easy to drift into pure tinkering with no one to sell to. *"You might just be playing at the wave."*
- **Example:** Bill Gates + Paul Allen at the frontier of microcomputers — saw a magazine, built a programming language for PC manufacturers (a small market of hobbyist-shop builders) → Microsoft.

**🚧 Dam** — an existing shared project everybody is doing what they can with, where new technology now allows the dam to be broken.
- **Where to go:** working alongside real businesses today. *"You're going to your local financial advisor and doing work with them to see where there's potentially pull in their given day."*
- **What to do:** observe → generate hypothesis from real-life observation → show them *"here's what I think your pull is and what we could build."* If they say *"oh my god yes,"* dam is bursting.
- **Why Rob prefers this:** less wave-uncertainty. You can know if you can get one across the line. Real businesses, real problems, helping real people accomplish real things.

**🌊 River** — fast or slow-moving existing flow; not deeply unpacked in this video.

### The 5-call sprint (the practical execution loop)

The repeatable mechanic for testing a hypothesis of pull:
1. **Write out your hypothesis of pull** using the 4-criteria framework above (project + unavoidability + options + limits)
2. **Talk to 5 potential customers** this week
3. **Analyze:** were any of them in the situation you hypothesized? Did they pull?
4. **Revise** the hypothesis from what you learned
5. **Run another 5** — repeat until you can predict who will pull and who won't

**Validation threshold:** *"You talk to five people and four of them buy, and you kind of know why the other one didn't."* That's a validated hypothesis of demand.

## Mental models / principles

- **What you think you're looking for is *product ideas*. What you're actually looking for is *demand*.** Most founders never make this distinction — and look for the wrong thing in the wrong places. — *Rob Snyder*
- **Pain points are not enough.** *"Life is just a series of pain points that we mostly do nothing about."* — *Rob Snyder*
- **"People who would benefit" are not enough.** ROI cases like *"I can increase your revenues by 20%"* land as *"cool, shut up."* — *Rob Snyder*
- **"People who want your product" are not enough.** *"Has anybody ever wanted an ERP?"* — *Rob Snyder*
- **The project is upstream of the product.** Their to-do list determines whether your product is relevant. — *Rob Snyder*
- **Most advice is designed to make you feel comfortable, not successful.** Discovery interviews feel productive — *"the person might even thank you, you might leave with things that feel useful."* That's the trap. — *Rob Snyder*
- **There's an "uncanny valley" between LARPing-as-founder and being productive.** Pitch competitions are pure LARP. Discovery interviews are in the uncanny valley. *"Things that feel productive but actually generally aren't."* — *Rob Snyder*
- **The bare minimum is the sale. The real objective is retention.** *"It would be weird if they stopped paying you. It would be weird if they didn't upgrade or refer."* — *Rob Snyder*
- **You're trying to retain the *right* customers.** Early on you'll sell to wrong-fit ones; that's part of learning who the right ones are. — *RC (host)*

## Tactics & playbooks

- **Don't go to the whiteboard.** *"If you don't have an idea, don't go to the whiteboard. Go to where your customers are. Spend a week there."*
- **Become your customer.** Cited examples: HBS grad became an EMT for 6 months; greenhouse designer bought a greenhouse.
- **For waves: build for yourself first.** Tinker with the new technology to solve your own frontier-problem; then show it to others at the frontier.
- **For dams: work alongside a real business for a week.** Observe their day. Generate the pull hypothesis from observation, not from a spreadsheet of pain points.
- **Use the "It would be weird if..." test** to gut-check unavoidability.
- **Use the Janky Product Test** to assess demand intensity. If they wouldn't take the embarrassing V1 today, the pull isn't there yet.
- **Run 5-call sprints.** Five calls/week, write hypothesis, debug after each batch.
- **When pitching, lead with demand, not supply.** *"I need to be talking about demand and where their options aren't good. Given that, here's what I propose."*
- **If discovery interviews are getting you compliments, you're in the trap.** Real selling exposes real demand; interviews invite social politeness.
- **Avoid "design partner" framings.** When people hear "design partner," they switch into "fun project, no risk" mode — exactly the wrong frame for getting real signal.

## Counterintuitive claims

- **"Discovery interviews are arrogant."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: 20-100 discovery interviews is the gold-standard early step (Steve Blank, JTBD, etc.). His argument: *"It is the height of arrogance to say 'I don't really know this industry, but I want to build a game-changing product, and I'm going to figure it out from 30-minute open-ended conversations.'"* Unless you're Bob Moesta, it doesn't work. Plus they're "not serious" — *"if your life depended on it, you wouldn't just do discovery interviews. You'd go work alongside, get your hands dirty."*
- **"Design partners are usually a trap."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: 3 design partners = strong early validation. His argument: when people hear "design partner," the part of their brain saying *"I have an actual thing I'm trying to accomplish"* switches OFF and *"I'll have fun working with a startup, no risk to me"* switches ON. The feedback you get is irrelevant — they're optimizing for a fun ego-project, not a real outcome. *"It's often harder to get someone to be a design partner than to get them to buy."*
- **"Pain points are not demand."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: find a 10/10 pain point, you've found a business. His argument: *"Life is just a series of pain points we mostly do nothing about."* Demand requires *action*, not just suffering.
- **"Most advice is designed to make you feel comfortable, not successful."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: most advice is well-meaning. His argument: well-meaning ≠ effective. The interview-and-validate cycle has a built-in feel-good reward that masks lack of progress.
- **"The objective is not the sale, it's retention. The sale is the bare minimum."** — *Rob Snyder*. Conventional view: getting to revenue = winning. His argument: revenue without retention = leakage. *"What we're really designing for is the retention experience — but to get there, the only door is they bought something."*
- **"You can't tell a real wave from noise without going to the frontier."** — *Rob Snyder* (responding to Bokeardia's question about metaverse vs real waves). His emerging hypothesis: things that fail at the frontier *don't even find businesses to sell to* — they're tinkering with technology, not tinkering with technology *for businesses*. Microsoft sold programming languages to PC-manufacturer hobbyist-shops; metaverse mostly didn't have anyone specific to sell to.

## Real-life examples cited

- **Plaid → Venmo.** Plaid pivoted from consumer budgeting apps (where users had pain but not action) to B2B fintech infrastructure. Venmo had an *unavoidable* project (connect to every bank to send P2P money) with bad options (build it themselves). They ripped Plaid's hacky early integration out of their hands. *Demand was so intense the product didn't have to be polished.*
- **Bill Gates / Paul Allen + Microsoft.** At the frontier of microcomputers — saw the first PC in a magazine, recognized that PC manufacturers would need programming languages. Built BASIC for them. Wave-tinkering with a *specific market of customers* at the frontier (not generalized "computer in every home" tinkering).
- **HBS grad → EMT.** To understand the demand, became one for 6 months.
- **Greenhouse designer → bought a greenhouse.** Same logic — to understand demand, become the customer.
- **Bob Moesta** — JTBD creator, the *only* person Rob has seen do genuinely good discovery interviews. *"He can see into your soul in a discovery."* Everyone else who claims to do good discovery interviews is fooling themselves.
- **The "motorcycle repair shop CRM" anti-pattern.** The vertical-SaaS-pitch trap: showing a small business their inefficient paper system, demoing your slick alternative, getting *"yeah it'd be so much better"* — and then *"do you want to buy?"* / *"yeah, not really."* Force-feeding them broccoli they don't want.

## Open questions

- **What does "becoming your customer" look like for a B2B SaaS sales tool aimed at SMBs?** The EMT / greenhouse examples are operational/manual jobs. What's the equivalent for a "SMB owner running a 20-person business" Melissa?
- **Wave or Dam for the founder's current direction?** B2B SaaS sales-org tooling at SMB scale is more dam-shaped (existing shared project: book meetings, manage pipeline). But there's wave-shape too (AI is transforming what's possible). Worth deciding consciously.
- **Is the "5-call sprint" compatible with the founder's proposed paid-ads variant?** 5 calls/week from cold DMs vs 5 calls/week from paid-ad-booked meetings → does the analysis differ? Probably yes — see TENSIONS.md note on inbound vs outbound signal differences.
- **Rob's design-partner critique — does it apply to "I'll talk to a friend who runs a small business" early conversations?** Probably not, if framed as sales rather than partnership. Worth being explicit.
- **What's the River?** Rob mentioned the wave/dam/river framework but didn't unpack the river archetype. Future-self note: chase down the original blog post for the third archetype.
