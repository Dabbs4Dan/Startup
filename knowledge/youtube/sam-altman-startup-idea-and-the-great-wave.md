# Sam Altman — How to come up with a great startup idea

> **Caveat:** This is a curated 3:21 clip from a longer talk (full video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4SlNgM4PjvQ). The full source may contain caveats and qualifiers that don't appear here.

## Core thesis
Don't start a startup without a real idea — passionate conviction in a specific problem is the load-bearing input. Original thought is rare; most founders copy, and copies lose. The two reliable origin patterns are **solving your own problem** and **noticing a technology wave early enough to surf it**.

## Frameworks

### The Great Wave
- **What:** A massive, identifiable technology shift visible "in the distance" before it crashes over the market. You can't *create* a wave (too capital-intensive for a startup) — but you can spot one early and ride it before competitors take it seriously.
- **How to spot one:** It looks like a toy or doesn't seem to matter. University students are unusually good at this — they're closer to the leading edge and not yet trained out of it.
- **Why it works:** Great companies cluster in time around waves, because the same shift creates the same opportunity for many founders simultaneously.
- **Examples (from source):**
  - **Internet wave (mid-to-late 1990s):** Amazon, Google, Yahoo
  - **Mobile wave (post-iPhone, 2007–2008):** Uber, Airbnb, Snapchat, Instagram, WhatsApp
  - **The implicit question:** what's the wave starting right now?

### Two reliable idea-origination patterns
- **Pattern 1: Solve your own problem.** Not universal, but a "very high percentage" of transformational companies start this way.
- **Pattern 2: Surf someone else's wave early.** Notice the shift before others take it seriously, then build with conviction.

## Mental models / principles
- **Wait to have a good idea before you start a startup.** Founders who start without one and "cast around" feel pressure to manufacture something — and it doesn't work. — *Sam Altman*
- **Original thought is really hard.** Most people copy what someone else is doing. — *Sam Altman*
- **The first mover in a copy wave usually wins.** "If you have one idea that starts to work and you have 10,000 clones that follow it, none of you are going to be successful, and the person that started first that you all copied is very likely to win." — *Sam Altman*
- **Conviction beats consensus.** "You want to do something that is new and different — you want to think of an idea that's not the same idea everybody else is working on." — *Sam Altman*
- **Clusters of greatness come from clusters of vision.** When a wave hits, multiple founders independently see it; that's why Amazon, Google, and Yahoo started near each other in time, and why mobile gave us Uber/Airbnb/Instagram/WhatsApp/Snapchat in a tight window.

## Tactics & playbooks
- Before starting a company, validate that you have **passionate conviction** in a specific idea — not just an appetite to be a founder.
- Audit your own life for problems that aren't being solved well. Founder-solves-own-problem is the most common origin story for transformational companies.
- Look for waves where conventional opinion still says **"that's a toy"** or **"that doesn't matter."** That's the entry window.
- If you're a student or close to a frontier, weight your own conviction *higher* than market consensus on what matters.
- Ask explicitly: "what is the wave starting right now, and where is the cluster of companies that's going to ride it?"

## Counterintuitive claims
- **Don't start a startup without an idea.** — *Sam Altman*. Conventional view: just start something and iterate; ideas are cheap, execution matters. His argument: starting without conviction creates pressure to manufacture an idea, which doesn't work — passionate belief in a specific problem is what carries founders through the long middle.
- **Copying a working idea is disastrous, even if the idea is great.** — *Sam Altman*. Conventional view: ride the trend; if it's working for them, it can work for you. His argument: 10,000 clones means almost everyone loses to the original. The year after Facebook: 1000+ social networks. The year after Instagram: 1000+ photo apps. The year he joined Helion: 10,000 photo startups, 1 nuclear fusion startup.
- **You should look for things that *currently look like toys*.** — *Sam Altman*. Conventional view: serious markets attract serious capital and serious founders. His argument: real waves are missed by most because they look unimportant at the start; that's exactly when you have time to get on your surfboard.

## Open questions
- This clip says **what** to look for (own problem + wave) but skips **how to validate** that what you're seeing is actually a wave vs. a fad. The full talk likely covers this.
- The "10,000 photo startups, 1 fusion startup" anecdote argues against crowded spaces — but Helion is also a famously capital-intensive bet. How do early-stage founders without nuclear-fusion-level capital pick between "uncrowded but capital-heavy" and "crowded but accessible"?
- Sam mentions students are particularly good at noticing waves. Does this advantage transfer to non-students who deliberately keep close to a frontier? Or is there something specific about being not-yet-trained-out-of-it that you can't manufacture?
